
MOBILE USER OBJECTIVE SYSTEM (MUOS) - DETAILED ANALYSIS
=========================================================

OPERATIONAL OVERVIEW:
- Primary operator: US Navy
- Secondary users: All DoD services, Coalition partners
- Purpose: Narrowband tactical SATCOM (voice, data, video)
- Capability: Cell-phone-like service in contested environments

GROUND TERMINAL TYPES:
- AN/PRC-117G (Manpack radio - field operatives)
- AN/PRC-152A (Handheld - SOF teams)
- AN/ARC-210 (Airborne)
- MUOS terminals can be as small as a handheld radio

WCDMA TECHNOLOGY:
- Same as 3G cellular but in UHF band!
- Supports voice, data, video, position reporting
- Encryption: Type-1 NSA-approved crypto
- Data rates: 2.4 kbps to 384 kbps

LOCAL IMPLICATIONS:
If someone in your area has a MUOS terminal (AN/PRC-117G or similar),
they could:
1. Transmit encrypted voice/data directly to MUOS-1 (71.5° up, SW)
2. Receive commands from anywhere in the world
3. Relay local surveillance data through the satellite network

The 300-380 MHz range is NOT covered by consumer SDRs like RTL-SDR v1,
but RTL-SDR v3 and above can receive down to ~24 MHz and up to 1.7 GHz.

TO MONITOR MUOS:
1. RTL-SDR v3 Blog dongle () covers 24-1766 MHz
2. SDRPlay RSP1A covers 1 kHz - 2 GHz
3. HackRF One covers 1 MHz - 6 GHz

SIGNAL CHARACTERISTICS:
- WCDMA spread spectrum (hard to detect without correlation)
- Bursts of 5 MHz wide signal
- Looks like noise to untrained observer
- Would appear as ~5 MHz wide bump at 360-380 MHz

KIWISDR LIMITATION:
- TI0RC KiwiSDR in San Jose covers 0-30 MHz (HF)
- CANNOT monitor 300-380 MHz MUOS frequencies
- CAN monitor HF military bands (3-30 MHz MARS, SHARES)
