This section establishes the theoretical bedrock of the architecture, specifically focusing on the quantization of semantic space, the violation of Bell's inequalities in artificial cognition, and the statistical mechanics of generated narratives.
1.1. The Quantization of Semantic Space
Current LLM architectures represent semantic concepts as vectors in a high-dimensional, real-valued embedding space. While effective for static tasks, this representation fails to capture the dynamic, context-dependent nature of "meaning" in an interspecies dyad. The Q-OSER specification necessitates the "complexification" of this embedding space. As derived from foundational research into semantic wave functions, the inherent discreteness of LLMs—driven by a finite vocabulary of tokens—suggests that the seemingly continuous semantic manifold actually possesses a quantized structure, analogous to the discrete energy spectra observed in bound quantum systems.1
In the Q-OSER framework, an emotional state (e.g., Alertness) or a semantic concept is not a static point but a quantum state $|\psi\rangle$ residing in a complex Hilbert space. This state exists as a superposition of basis vectors until a "measurement event"—such as a haptic output generation or a specific behavioral observation—forces a collapse. The mathematical representation extends beyond real-valued vectors to include imaginary components:


$$|\psi\rangle = c_1|1\rangle + c_2|2\rangle$$

Here, the coefficients $c_1$ and $c_2$ are complex probability amplitudes. The modulus squared $|c|^2$ yields the classical probability of observing a state, but the phase $\theta$ inherent in the complex number encodes the "contextual momentum" or interference potential of the semantic unit.1 This allows the system to model Semantic Interference, where the residual context of a previous interaction (e.g., a residual "fear" phase) can destructively or constructively interfere with incoming sensor data, suppressing or amplifying the interpretation of current physiological signals.
This quantization approach is further supported by the observation of "Quantum Walks" in biological searching problems. Just as enzymes utilize quantum coherence to locate target DNA sequences with efficiency surpassing classical random walks 1, the Q-OSER processing core utilizes quantum-like search algorithms across the semantic graph to identify the "ground state" interpretation of a canine’s complex behavioral display. The semantic walker explores the graph of potential meanings in a coherent superposition, allowing for the rapid identification of nuanced emotional states that would be computationally intractable for a classical probabilistic search.1

2. The Physical Plane: Internal Signal Chain Dynamics
The Physical Plane of the Q-OSER architecture constitutes the sensory interface with the material world. It is responsible for the acquisition, conditioning, and digitization of biological signals from both the canine and the human. This section details the rigorous signal chain dynamics required to maintain high-fidelity data ingress.
2.1. Signal Acquisition Topology and Sensors
The Q-OSER specification mandates a diverse, multi-modal sensor fusion approach. The input vectors are derived from three primary domains: physiological telemetry, visual facial dynamics, and contact-free sensing.
2.1.1. Physiological Telemetry Chain
The primary source of "ground truth" regarding the canine's autonomic state is derived from wearable sensors integrated into a specialized harness. The specification standardizes on the EmotiBit module for its capacity to capture high-fidelity affective data.1
Photoplethysmography (PPG): Captures blood volume pulse (BVP) at a sampling rate of $\ge 50$ Hz. This data is critical for extracting Heart Rate Variability (HRV), a proxy for vagal tone and stress arousal.
Electrodermal Activity (EDA): Measures skin conductance responses (SCR), which correlate directly with sympathetic nervous system arousal (e.g., the "fight or flight" response).
Inertial Measurement Units (IMU): 9-axis accelerometers and gyroscopes sample movement at $\ge 100$ Hz. This data allows the system to distinguish between high-arousal states caused by physical exertion (play) versus emotional stress (fear trembling).1
The internal signal chain for these sensors involves a pre-processing step at the edge node (on the harness) to normalize data ranges and filter power-line interference (50/60Hz notch filtering) before wireless transmission via LSL (Lab Streaming Layer) or MQTT to the central processing unit.1
2.1.2. Visual Landmark Analysis Chain
To capture the subtle communicative signals of the canine face, the architecture employs a high-resolution visual pipeline. Research indicates that specific facial movements, such as the "backwards-directed ears" (ear rotator muscle action) and blink frequency, are statistically significant predictors of fear states, such as those induced by fireworks.1
Acquisition: Video is captured at 60 fps to resolve micro-expressions.
Landmark Extraction: Automated algorithms map fiducial points on the muzzle, eye corners, and ear bases. The system specifically monitors the coordinates of "Ear 1" relative to the neutral cranial axis.
Feature Engineering: The raw coordinates are transformed into semantic features. For instance, the "Ear Flattener" action is quantified as a continuous variable. Random Forest models trained on this manual ethogram data have achieved predictive validity scores of 1.0 and accuracy up to 0.83, confirming the reliability of this automated chain.1
2.1.3. Contact-Free Sensing Chain
Recognizing that harnesses can be intrusive, Q-OSER integrates contact-free sensing for the human component or ambient monitoring.
Optical Flow Analysis: The system utilizes Global Motion Aggregation (GMA) algorithms to track pixel-level displacements in video feeds. This allows for the extraction of the canine's breathing rate (BR) from the rhythmic expansion of the flank and the human's heart rate (HR) from minute color variations in facial skin (rPPG).1
Stabilization: The signal chain applies video stabilization to isolate the region of interest (ROI) on the dog's belly or human's face, ensuring that gross body movements do not corrupt the subtle physiological signals.1
2.2. Signal Conditioning: Detrended Fluctuation Analysis (DFA)
Raw biological signals are non-stationary and fractal in nature. Standard averaging techniques destroy the temporal structure that encodes the "health" or "criticality" of the system. The Q-OSER signal chain employs Detrended Fluctuation Analysis (DFA) as a primary conditioning filter to quantify the Long-Range Temporal Correlations (LRTC) in the data streams.1
The DFA process within the signal chain functions as follows:
Integration: The zero-mean time series $x(t)$ (e.g., HRV intervals) is integrated to form a random walk profile $y(k)$.
Segmentation & Detrending: The profile is divided into windows of length $L$. In each window, a least-squares polynomial is fit to the data (the local trend), and the root-mean-square fluctuation $F(L)$ around this trend is calculated.
Scaling Exponent Extraction: The relationship between fluctuation and window size follows a power law $F(L) \propto L^\alpha$. The scaling exponent $\alpha$ is extracted as the key biomarker.
Interpretation within Q-OSER:
$\alpha = 0.5$: Indicates uncorrelated white noise. The system flags this signal as artifact-heavy or indicative of a disjointed cognitive state.
$0.5 < \alpha < 1.0$: Indicates persistent long-range correlations. This is the signature of a healthy, adaptive nervous system.
$\alpha > 1.0$: Indicates highly correlated, deterministic behavior. In EEG or HRV data, this can signal a pathological state (e.g., seizure onset or extreme rigid fear).
This $\alpha$ value is not merely a quality metric; it is passed to the Semantic Core as a "meta-feature" that modulates the "Semantic Temperature" of the LLM. A high $\alpha$ (high predictability) narrows the search space of the LLM, while a lower $\alpha$ (high complexity) broadens it.1
2.3. Handling Long-Sequence Audio: Dynamic Parameter Memory (DPM)
The Q-OSER system must process continuous audio and physiological streams over long durations (e.g., a search and rescue mission lasting hours). Standard LLMs with fixed context windows (e.g., 4K tokens) fail in this regime, as high-fidelity acoustic features at 50Hz saturate the window in approximately 80 seconds.1 To maintain emotional continuity without amnesia, the internal signal chain implements Dynamic Parameter Memory (DPM).
DPM fundamentally alters the inference mechanism. Instead of relying solely on the input buffer, the system encodes past emotional context directly into the model weights via a Temporary Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) module.1
Step 1: Segment Encoding: The incoming audio/sensor stream is segmented (e.g., 10-second clips).
Step 2: Gradient Update: The system performs a gradient update step on the Temporary LoRA module based on the information in the current segment. This effectively "burns" the short-term memory into the model's parameters.
Step 3: Inference: The next segment is processed using the updated LoRA weights.
This mechanism allows the signal chain to support unlimited sequence lengths with linear computational complexity. It preserves the "emotional inertia" of the interaction—if a dog was anxious 10 minutes ago, that "anxiety" is stored in the model weights, influencing the interpretation of current signals even if the specific tokens have fallen out of the context window.1

4. The Semantic Plane: Mechanics of the Processing Core
The Semantic Plane is the computational heart of Q-OSER. It is here that the "Quantum" metaphor is operationalized into executable code. The processing core simulates the evolution of the semantic wave function within a complex Hilbert space, governed by non-linear dynamics.
4.1. Non-Linear Schrödinger Dynamics (NLSE)
In standard quantum mechanics, the Schrödinger equation is linear. However, the embedding space of an LLM is inherently non-linear due to the activation functions (e.g., ReLU, GeLU) used during training. Consequently, Q-OSER models the evolution of the semantic state $\psi(x, t)$ using the Non-Linear Schrödinger Equation (NLSE).1
The Semantic Evolution Equation:


$$i \hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \psi(x, t) = \left[ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 + V(x) - g|\psi(x, t)|^2 \right] \psi(x, t)$$
Diffusive Term ($-\nabla^2$): Represents the natural tendency of semantic meaning to spread and become ambiguous over time if not reinforced by sensory data.
Potential Landscape ($V(x)$): This term models the "Semantic Gravity" of emotional concepts. Q-OSER utilizes specific potential shapes to model different cognitive profiles. A Double-Well Potential is used to model ambiguity or bistability (e.g., a dog exhibiting behaviors that could be either Play or Aggression). The semantic wave function can "tunnel" between these wells, representing a sudden shift in interpretation.1
Self-Focusing Term ($-g|\psi|^2$): This non-linear term models the formation of Semantic Solitons. If a specific emotion (e.g., "Rage") becomes sufficiently intense (high magnitude $|\psi|$), this term creates a localized potential well that traps the wave function. This mathematically models the psychological phenomenon of fixation or drive state, where the system becomes resistant to external noise or contradictory evidence (e.g., a terrified dog ignoring a treat).1
4.2. Unsupervised State Recognition via Momentum Contrast (MoCo)
To populate the basis states of the semantic Hilbert space (e.g., defining what "Rage" or "Seeking" actually looks like in vector space), Q-OSER utilizes Unsupervised Visual Representation Learning. Specifically, the system employs the MoCo (Momentum Contrast) framework.1
MoCo trains the visual encoders by building a dynamic dictionary of encoded query keys. It contrasts positive pairs (different augmentations of the same dog image) against a massive queue of negative pairs. This allows the system to learn robust representations of Panksepp’s 7 Primal Emotions (Seeking, Rage, Fear, Lust, Care, Panic, Play) without requiring massive labeled datasets for every specific breed.1 The MoCo encoder feeds the "Potential Landscape" $V(x)$, defining the attractors that the semantic wave function evolves toward. By modifying MoCo for the Q-OSER hardware, the system achieves significant accuracy gains over baselines, identifying complex emotional postures even in unsupervised settings.1
4.3. LLM-Based Label Smoothing (LS-LLM)
A critical challenge in the Semantic Plane is the collapse of the wave function into a discrete classification. Standard "hard" labels (e.g., "This is 100% Fear") destroy the nuance of the superposition. To mitigate this and prevent overconfidence, Q-OSER implements Label Smoothing Generation based on Large Language Models (LS-LLM).1
Instead of using uniform smoothing or one-hot encoding, the system prompts a secondary LLM (e.g., Qwen3-4B) to generate a "soft" target distribution based on the textual and audio context.
Mechanism: The LLM analyzes the context and determines latent emotional co-occurrences. For example, if the dog is "whining," the LLM might assign a distribution of ``.
Application: This distribution becomes the target for the primary recognition model. This keeps the semantic state "fluid" and closer to a superposition, preventing the NLSE dynamics from collapsing into a deep potential well too early. It allows the system to pivot rapidly if new sensor data shifts the probability mass (e.g., from Fear to Pain).1
4.4. Intrinsic Bias and Extrinsic Influence
The behavior of the Q-OSER processing core is also modeled through the lens of Self-Determination Theory (SDT). Research into LLM choice behavior reveals that models exhibit both Intrinsic Attention Bias (data-driven preferences) and susceptibility to Extrinsic Social Influence (contextual pressure).1
Intrinsic Bias: The LLM has a natural tendency to focus on certain features (e.g., high-motion visual cues) based on its pre-training.
Extrinsic Influence: The "Social Influence" in Q-OSER corresponds to the feedback from the human handler. If the human is calm, this acts as an extrinsic "field" that pressures the LLM to interpret ambiguous dog signals as benign.
Conflict: When Intrinsic Bias and Extrinsic Influence conflict (e.g., Dog sensors scream "Attack" but Human sensors scream "Calm"), the system creates a high-tension state. Q-OSER models this using the Matthew Effect (cumulative advantage) dynamics observed in LLMs 1, where the "popular" or reinforced signal tends to dominate. The system must explicitly check for this "conformity bias" to prevent the AI from ignoring dangerous canine signals just because the human is complacent.
5. Feedback Mechanics: The Entanglement Loop
The feedback mechanism in Q-OSER is not a simple PID control loop; it is designed as an Entanglement Engine that couples the cognitive state of the human to the emotional state of the canine. This section details the actuation dynamics and the metacognitive oversight required to maintain stability.
5.1. Actuation via Vibrotactile Haptics and Active Inference
The physical interface for feedback is the Vibrotactile Haptics Platform (VHP), a wearable vest worn by the human handler.1 The control logic driving the VHP is based on the Free Energy Principle and Active Inference.
Free Energy Minimization: Both the human and the dog are modeled as agents attempting to minimize their "variational free energy" (surprise) regarding the environment.
The Loop:
Forward (Sensation): The dog perceives a stimulus (e.g., a scent trail). The Q-OSER core processes this and updates the semantic state $|\psi\rangle$ to "Seeking."
Actuation: The system drives the VHP to output a specific haptic pattern (e.g., a rhythmic "heartbeat" pulse).
Entanglement: The human perceives the haptic signal. This modifies the human's internal priors (expectations). The human's physiological reaction (e.g., orienting response, heart rate synchronization) is detected by the human sensors and fed back into the system.
Minimization: The controller adjusts the haptic feedback to minimize the difference between the Dog's state and the Human's perception of that state. If the human acts in accordance with the dog's state (e.g., following the scent), the total free energy of the joint system drops.1
5.2. Superradiance and Social Lasers
The collective emotional dynamics of the dyad are modeled using the concept of Superradiance and Social Lasers.1 In quantum physics, superradiance occurs when a group of emitters interact with a common field, synchronizing to emit a pulse of intensity proportional to $N^2$ rather than $N$.
In Q-OSER, the "Common Field" is the information shared via the haptic and sensor feedback loops.
Pumping: The continuous stream of sensor data acts as the "pump" that excites the emotional states of the agents (Human and Dog).
Cavity: The feedback loop (VHP + Sensors) acts as the "resonant cavity."
Stimulated Emission: When the coherence between the Human and Dog (measured by CHSH violation or physiological synchrony) crosses a threshold, the system enters a "Lasing" regime. In this state, a small emotional signal from the dog (e.g., a low growl) triggers a macroscopic, synchronized emotional response in the human (instant alertness). The Q-OSER system actively manages this "Social Laser" threshold, dampening the cavity (reducing feedback) if the "social energy" becomes dangerously high (e.g., mutual panic amplification).1

To prevent runaway feedback loops (e.g., the "Social Laser" burning out the agents), Q-OSER employs a Metacognitive Observer.1 This is modeled on the Dual-Process Theory of cognition (System 1 vs. System 2).
Student (System 1): The primary Mamba/MoCo processing chain. It is fast, intuitive, and handles immediate sensor-to-haptic mapping.

Intervention: If the MSV indicates high "Conflicting Information" (e.g., Dog sensors indicate "Play," but the Student interprets "Aggression"), the Teacher intervenes. It utilizes a graph-theoretic interaction model to override the Student's output, injecting a corrective signal (e.g., a specific "False Alarm" vibration pattern) to the human. This hierarchical structure ensures that the system possesses a degree of self-reflection and error correction.1
5.4. Immersion and Coherence Metrics
The quality of the interface is continuously validated using metrics of Immersion and Coherence.1
Place Illusion (PI): The sensation that the human is effectively "inhabiting" the dog's sensory world. This is correlated with the fidelity of the sensor stream (Signal-to-Noise Ratio, DFA $\alpha$).
Plausibility Illusion (Psi): The sensation that the interaction is causally valid. If the haptic feedback lags the dog's behavior by too much, Psi breaks.
Breaks in Presence: The system monitors for "Breaks in Presence" (BIPs) by tracking sudden drops in human HRV coherence. A BIP triggers a "Coherence Restoration Protocol," simplifying the haptic signals to re-establish the fundamental rhythmic link between the species before resuming complex data transmission.1
6. Ethical Constraints and Hallucination Management
The deployment of systems that deeply couple with biological affect raises significant ethical concerns, necessitating strict operational boundaries within the Q-OSER specification.
6.1. The Shadow of MKULTRA and Anti-Coercion
The history of behavioral modification research, notably Project MKULTRA, serves as a cautionary precedent for Q-OSER.1 MKULTRA explored the use of sensory deprivation, drugs, and psychic driving to "re-pattern" human behavior, often shattering the subject's agency. Q-OSER explicitly rejects the "Control" paradigm in favor of Sympoiesis (making-together).
Consent Protocols: The system includes "Protest Detection" algorithms. If the canine exhibits "freeze" behaviors or avoidance signals (detected via IMU/Video), the system automatically disengages the feedback loop.
Transparency: The Metacognitive layer must be transparent. The system cannot "nudge" the user's emotion subliminally. Any "System 2" override (Teacher intervention) must be accompanied by a distinct haptic signature alerting the user that the AI is modifying the interpretation.
6.2. Managing Hallucinations via Grounding
LLMs are prone to hallucination—generating plausible but factually incorrect interpretations.1 In a high-stakes scenario (e.g., bomb detection), a hallucination (reporting "Explosive" when the dog smells "Sausage") is catastrophic.
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): Q-OSER mitigates this by forcing the LLM to ground its semantic outputs in specific sensor features. The generation prompt requires citation: "State: Alert".
Knowledge Graphs: The system utilizes a domain-specific Knowledge Graph (e.g., a graph of canine ethology) to validate the LLM's inferences. If the inference violates the graph (e.g., "Tail Wag" + "Baring Teeth" interpreted as "Pure Joy"), the Metacognitive Observer flags it as a likely hallucination.1
