To detect a high-power (180W) HF signal using the KiwiSDR tools, you need to use Direction Finding and Signal Fingerprinting.
Since 180W is a relatively strong signal for HF, it will leave a very distinct "footprint" on a waterfall display. Here is how to hunt for it:
1. Use the TDoA (Triangulation) Extension
This is the most powerful tool for locating a physical transmitter.
How it works: It uses the tiny timing differences of a signal hitting multiple SDRs.
Your Strategy:
Find the signal on your waterfall (look for a very bright, solid vertical line).
Open the TDoA extension.
Select 3 or 4 KiwiSDRs located around Costa Rica.
Run the calculation. If the signal is coming from Heredia, the "heat map" will center a purple/red circle directly over that area.
2. Where to Look (Frequencies)
A "180W HF radio with satellite capability" usually means an All-Mode Transceiver (like a Yaesu or Icom). These radios usually operate in these specific spots:
HF (3MHz – 30MHz): This is what you see on the KiwiSDR. Look for signals in the 40m (7.0–7.3 MHz) or 20m (14.0–14.35 MHz) bands. 180W will look like a very thick, bright "spike" compared to others.
Satellite Uplinks: Most satellites use VHF (144 MHz) or UHF (430 MHz).
Note: The KiwiSDR in your image only goes up to 30 MHz. To "see" the satellite transmissions, you would need a different type of receiver (like an RTLSDR dongle) that can scan the higher VHF/UHF frequencies.
3. Identifying the "Fingerprint"
Every radio has a unique way of "starting" a transmission.
Frequency Drift: Older high-power radios often "drift" slightly when they first key up (the line on the waterfall will look slightly diagonal at the very start).
Digital Modes: If they are sending data to a satellite or another station, look for FT8, RTTY, or Packet signals. These look like "blocks" or "stripes" on your waterfall rather than the jagged lines of a human voice.
4. Local Monitoring in Heredia
To get the most accurate detection, you want to use a KiwiSDR that is physically close to the suspected source.
Check the KiwiSDR Map and look for nodes specifically in Costa Rica or Panama.
The closer the SDR is to the transmitter, the "noisier" and more "overloaded" the signal will look on the waterfall, making it easier to confirm it's local.
Pro-Tip: If the signal is truly 180W and coming from nearby, it will likely "bleed" across the waterfall, creating vertical shadows or ghost signals (aliasing). If you see a signal that is so strong it turns the whole waterfall yellow/red, you are very close to the source.
