Latent Infrastructure Mapping for SSC CASINO, DARPA Blackjack, and SDA PWSA in Latin America: Orchestration vs. Physicality
1. The Geopolitics of Commercial Orchestration and the Abstraction of Space Infrastructure
The contemporary architecture of national security space assets is undergoing a radical, structural paradigm shift away from monolithic, government-owned and government-operated ground segments toward proliferated, commercially orchestrated networks. The central investigative hypothesis of this exhaustive analytical report addresses the profound "orchestration versus physicality" paradox inherent in modern defense architectures. As advanced program offices—most notably the Space Systems Command (SSC), the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA), and the Space Development Agency (SDA)—increasingly rely on commercial orchestration platforms such as Microsoft Azure Orbital, AWS Ground Station, and mission-agnostic integrators like Loft Orbital, a deliberate and highly effective abstraction layer is created.   

This software-defined abstraction layer intentionally obscures the physical control nodes, jurisdictional data sovereignty boundaries, and the ultimate beneficial ownership of the underlying critical infrastructure. Under the guise of "cloud-orchestrated ground networks" or "space infrastructure as a service," military and intelligence data is routed dynamically through civilian teleports and commercial holding companies. This model presents unique challenges for infrastructure mapping, particularly in Latin American jurisdictions characterized by varying degrees of corporate transparency, opaque real estate holding structures, and complex regulatory environments governing telecommunications and spectrum usage. The primary unknown driving this research is whether the celebrated "no dedicated program-branded site" model championed by proliferated Low Earth Orbit (pLEO) constellations actually serves to mask program-controlled infrastructure hosted under commercial leaseback arrangements, local franchise agreements, or shell entities that may possess hidden affiliations with non-state actors, historical defense consortiums, or religious land-holding organizations.   

To systematically dismantle this abstraction layer, this research applies an escalated methodology of corporate forensics, technical signal intelligence (SIGINT) correlation, and contract archaeology, focusing specifically on the ground segment topology located in Costa Rica, Chile, and Brazil. The analysis scrutinizes the LeoLabs Space Radar facility in Guanacaste, Costa Rica, testing its operational boundaries and corporate veil against local telecommunications law. It maps the physical footprint and payload manifests of Loft Orbital's "Yet Another Mission" (YAM) satellite series, tracing their data pathways through South American commercial aggregators. Furthermore, it investigates the deep historical lineage of Italian space infrastructure in Latin America, tracing the legacy of the SICRAL military communications network to modern teleport joint ventures managed by Telespazio and Leonardo.   

Throughout this analysis, a strict Confidence Rating System is applied to all findings. Assessments designated as GREEN are verified via primary sources, including corporate registry extracts, contract documents, or spectrum licenses. Assessments designated as AMBER represent secondary source correlations where technical data aligns with press or industry literature. Assessments designated as RED denote unverified but hypothesized connections that require further field verification or classified analysis. Ethical boundaries regarding the attribution of ownership to religious organizations without primary registry extraction, as well as strict adherence to International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR) concerning specific technical parameters of optical inter-satellite links, are maintained rigorously throughout the report.

2. The Costa Rica LeoLabs Node: Spectrum Sovereignty and Space Situational Awareness
The geographic positioning of space tracking infrastructure is dictated by the unforgiving laws of orbital mechanics. To maintain continuous custody of objects in Low Earth Orbit, particularly those utilizing low-inclination or equatorial orbits, terrestrial tracking stations must be positioned near the equator. The LeoLabs Costa Rica Space Radar, situated in the municipality of Filadelfia de Carrillo in the Guanacaste province, was developed explicitly to exploit this geographic advantage.   

Physical Operationalization and Technical Licensing Boundaries
On April 22, 2021, LeoLabs officially declared the Costa Rica Space Radar to be fully operational, culminating a rapid deployment cycle that saw the facility break ground just nine months prior. This phased-array radar system is described within industry literature as the most advanced commercial space radar globally, capable of tracking active satellites and orbital debris as minute as two centimeters in diameter. The facility closes a critical geographical gap in global Space Situational Awareness (SSA), providing unprecedented equatorial coverage that serves a broad customer base, including commercial satellite operators, defense agencies, and scientific organizations.   

However, the physical reality of a phased-array radar requires massive power draw and high-capacity fiber backhaul to process and transmit the vast quantities of telemetry and positional data generated by tracking thousands of LEO objects simultaneously. While the specific proprietary configurations of the power grid tap (grid versus hybrid solar) and the exact termination points of the fiber backhaul (whether relying on domestic carriers like ICE or routing directly to submarine cable landing stations) remain abstracted from public marketing materials, the legal and technical operating parameters are strictly defined by Costa Rican telecommunications law.

Technical forensic analysis of regulatory filings with the Superintendencia de Telecomunicaciones (SUTEL) reveals the precise operational and legal boundaries of the LeoLabs facility. SUTEL's technical recommendation, formalized in Oficio N° 07262-SUTEL-DGC-2024 and subsequently approved by the SUTEL Council in September 2024, explicitly authorizes the LeoLabs system to operate strictly as a radar system. The authorization grants a "permiso de uso de frecuencias" (frequency use permit) for a radar tracking system and data analysis for the space industry, governed under the regulations for non-commercial private telecommunications networks.   

Crucially, the facility transmits and receives (TX/RX) in the specific S-band frequencies of 2940 MHz and 2960 MHz. Confidence Rating: GREEN. The frequency authorization and its technical classification are thoroughly verified via primary SUTEL administrative records (Expediente Administrativo N° DNPT-074-2019-2). Within this authorization, SUTEL explicitly imposes international regulatory constraints, warning LeoLabs that the operation of this radar system is subject to the provisions of footnote 5.424A of the International Telecommunication Union (ITU) Radio Regulations. This ITU stipulation mandates that the operation of the radar system must not cause harmful interference to established radionavigation services, nor can LeoLabs claim legal protection from interference originating from those pre-existing systems.   

The distinction between a "Radar" authorization and an "Estación Terrena" (ground station) authorization in Costa Rican telecommunications law is not merely semantic; it represents a profound functional and geopolitical boundary. An Estación Terrena license implies the legal right to conduct bidirectional Telemetry, Tracking, and Command (TT&C) operations—the ability to send active commands to a satellite. The current primary source documentation limits LeoLabs entirely to active sensing and SSA radar operations. Therefore, based strictly on the current SUTEL regulatory posture, the LeoLabs Costa Rica facility cannot legally serve as a direct command uplink or fire control pivot for the SDA Tracking Layer or DARPA Blackjack assets. It functions purely as an observational node feeding precision-tracked object data (a catalog exceeding 16,000 objects) into a cloud-based RESTful API for secondary consumption by defense and commercial clients.   

Corporate Forensics: Opacity, Transparency, and the Poder Generalísimo
The abstraction of physical infrastructure is heavily reliant on the complexities of international corporate law and local commercial registration. An exhaustive forensic investigation into the Registro Nacional de Costa Rica identifies the specific operating entity for the Guanacaste radar as "LEOLABS SPACE LIMITADA". The entity operates under the distinct cédula jurídica (corporate identification number) 3-102-784732. The company was legally constituted via a notary act on June 25, 2019, and formally published in the official government registry, La Gaceta, in July 2019. The legally registered representative (representante legal) of the entity is Luis Alberto Mora Ortega, who holds the Costa Rican identity number 7-0127-0907. Official communications regarding telecommunications regulation are routed through designated local counsel via the email domain ciber-regulacion.co.cr.   

A highly critical vector of this investigation concerns the Costa Rican Transparency and Final Beneficial Owners Registry (Registro de Transparencia y Beneficiarios Finales, or RTBF). The RTBF was designed to pierce corporate veils and identify the ultimate human beneficiaries of corporate assets, serving as a bulwark against money laundering and the covert control of strategic infrastructure by foreign state or non-state actors. Historically, submitting the required annual declarations to the RTBF presented a structural bottleneck for foreign defense and aerospace contractors: the legal representative submitting the declaration was required to utilize a highly secure digital signature issued exclusively by the Central Bank of Costa Rica. This digital signature is strictly limited to Costa Rican nationals or permanent residents, theoretically preventing foreign executives from managing local holding companies without yielding a degree of control to local partners.   

However, deep analysis of recent legal and administrative shifts reveals a critical mechanism that re-establishes the corporate abstraction layer. In March 2026, the Directorate of Registration of Legal Entities of the National Registry issued Circular CPJ-002-2026. This new registry criterion explicitly facilitates the granting and registration of powers of attorney for the specific purpose of RTBF compliance. Under this newly established framework, a foreign board of directors or assembly of partners can formally execute a "poder generalísimo" (a general, overarching power of attorney). This legal instrument allows the foreign entity to designate a third party—such as local legal counsel or an accounting firm—who possesses the requisite Central Bank digital signature to execute the RTBF filings on their behalf, without that third party assuming broader operational control or beneficial ownership of the infrastructure.   

Confidence Rating: GREEN. The legal existence, exact corporate nomenclature, and designated representation of LEOLABS SPACE LIMITADA are verified by primary SUTEL resolutions and Registro Nacional publications. Furthermore, the regulatory maneuver enabling foreign holding companies to utilize a poder generalísimo to bypass local digital signature constraints for RTBF compliance is irrefutably confirmed via primary legal circulars and interpretations by major accounting networks such as BDO and KPMG. This legal mechanism provides the exact corporate veil required for U.S.-based defense contractors to operate critical infrastructure in Latin America while minimizing exposure to local partners. SUTEL records further confirm that LeoLabs requested strict confidentiality regarding their beneficial ownership information due to the sensitive nature of their business model and the strategic growth of their economic interest group (GIE), a request noted during their interactions with the regulatory body.   

The Investigation of Religious Organizational Land Holdings
A primary unknown driving the investigative hypothesis was whether the "no dedicated program-branded site" model utilizes land leasebacks, franchise agreements, or property proxy ownership via local religious organizations. Specifically, the inquiry targeted property holdings by "La Iglesia de Jesucristo de los Santos de los Últimos Días" (LDS) and the "Asociación de los Testigos de Jehová de Costa Rica" within the operational radius of the Filadelfia de Carrillo radar site.

Property title searches executed within the Guanacaste region confirm that the LDS church holds significant, well-documented real estate assets. Official registry publications identify properties such as Finca matrícula número 69172-F-000 in Liberia and various lots in Filadelfia that are directly owned or heavily mortgaged by the organization. Furthermore, the LDS organization actively participates in local municipal infrastructure development, evidenced by high-profile donations of educational furniture to the Escuela Central Filadelfia, an event involving the U.S. Ambassador to Costa Rica, demonstrating the organization's deep integration into the local civic fabric.   

Confidence Rating: RED (Negative Finding). Despite the confirmed geographical proximity of substantial LDS property holdings in the Filadelfia and wider Guanacaste region to the LeoLabs radar site, an exhaustive and rigorous review of the corporate entity LEOLABS SPACE LIMITADA and adjacent property titles yields absolutely no evidence of proxy ownership, leaseback franchising, or beneficial ownership exceeding the 25% threshold by any religious organization. In strict adherence to the ethical constraints governing this intelligence assessment, no attribution of ownership is made to these non-state actors. The corporate structure of the LeoLabs facility appears to be an entirely standard commercial subsidiary arrangement, fully capitalized by venture-backed U.S. parent entities, utilizing traditional commercial real estate acquisition methods devoid of religious organizational overlap.   

3. The Virtualization of the Ground Segment: Loft Orbital and Azure Integration
The paradigm of "space infrastructure as a service" represents the ultimate manifestation of the orchestration versus physicality paradox. Loft Orbital operates at the vanguard of this movement, functioning as an expansive abstraction layer that allows payloads from defense, intelligence, and commercial entities to ride on standardized satellite buses while being completely controlled via a unified software orchestration interface. Customers interact with their physical assets in space through "Cockpit," a mission-agnostic, proprietary mission control software that provides an intuitive web-based user interface and robust API integration. Loft manages all day-to-day flight operations, spectrum licensing, and satellite commissioning, allowing the customer—be it DARPA or the SDA—to focus entirely on data exploitation rather than satellite flying.   

The YAM Satellite Manifest and Defense Payload Integration
The physical reality of the Loft Orbital fleet centers on its standardized satellite buses, marketed under the "YAM" (Yet Another Mission) nomenclature. While Loft is essentially a software and integration layer, the physical metal in orbit is primarily manufactured by LeoStella, a joint venture between BlackSky and Thales Alenia Space, operating out of Tukwila, Washington. By utilizing commodity satellite buses kept in inventory and applying a universal payload adapter hub, Loft eliminates bus-level non-recurring engineering (NRE), drastically accelerating the timeline from contract award to orbital deployment.   

An analysis of the specific YAM missions reveals deep ties to U.S. national security programs, utilizing orbits that mathematically guarantee regular, predictable passes over Latin American ground infrastructure:

YAM-2 (NORAD ID: 48911): Launched on June 29, 2021, from the Air Force Eastern Test Range, YAM-2 achieved a low Earth orbit with an inclination of 97.648 degrees. Following several years of experimental operations, high-fidelity tracking elements indicate that the satellite's flight ended as it decayed from orbit on August 27, 2025.   

YAM-3 (NORAD ID: 48915): Launched concurrently with YAM-2 on June 30, 2021, aboard the SpaceX Transporter-2 rideshare mission, YAM-3 represented a critical milestone. Operating in a sun-synchronous orbit with an inclination of 97.69 degrees and an altitude ranging from 431.3 km to 437.5 km, YAM-3 was the first satellite LeoStella delivered to a customer other than BlackSky. Most significantly, YAM-3 hosted a demonstration payload for the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency's (DARPA) Blackjack satellite constellation program, operating alongside a commercial Internet of Things (IoT) payload for Eutelsat. The successful operation of the DARPA Blackjack payload on YAM-3 provided invaluable data on the capabilities of proliferated satellite constellations operating via shared commercial platforms. Furthermore, YAM-3 hosted demonstrations of AI-enabled edge-based processing by Scientific Systems Company, Inc. (SSCI) as part of the Prototype On-Orbit Experimental Testbed (POET) experiment for the Space Development Agency (SDA).   

YAM-5 (NORAD ID: 55076): Launched on January 3, 2023, via a SpaceX Falcon 9 from Cape Canaveral, YAM-5 operates in a circular, retrograde, sun-synchronous orbit with an inclination of 97.33 degrees and an altitude of approximately 456 km to 471 km. The YAM-5 satellite bus is 3-axis stabilized, achieves beam steering via body steering of the bus, and lacks active propulsion. It hosts a highly sophisticated array of experimental payloads, including NASA's MURI (a longwave infrared camera/radiometer), an S-band IoT payload operating between 2400-2483.5 MHz, and a dedicated experimental S-band payload transmitting in the 2240-2290 MHz band and receiving in the 2067-2110 MHz band. Crucially, it hosts the "RF Space Lab" for the French tech company Kinéis, a reconfigurable radio-frequency payload designed to accelerate the in-orbit validation of space software and innovative modulations.

Commercial Aggregators and Latin American Downlink Nodes
Loft Orbital aggressively markets its Cockpit software as being entirely "agnostic to ground stations". However, the physical reality of space communications dictates that when the YAM satellites pass over the Southern Hemisphere, the radio frequency (RF) energy must be captured by terrestrial antennas. To achieve this global reach without investing in dedicated capital infrastructure, Loft relies heavily on massive commercial cloud orchestration networks, primarily Microsoft Azure Orbital.   

With Azure Orbital, operators like Loft off-load the responsibility of deploying and maintaining ground stations. Microsoft handles the scheduling, digitization of the RF signal, and immediate routing into the Azure cloud for processing. However, Microsoft itself relies on an expansive partner ecosystem of physical ground station networks to provide the actual metal dishes.   

Confidence Rating: AMBER. While Loft Orbital's software layer is highly centralized in San Francisco and Toulouse, tracking analysis and the architectural realities of Azure Orbital strongly imply that when YAM-3 and YAM-5 traverse South America, their data is downlinked through aggregated commercial hubs heavily concentrated in Chile. The extreme southern latitude of Punta Arenas makes it one of the most strategically vital geographic locations globally for downloading data from polar and sun-synchronous orbiting satellites.   

AWS Ground Station (Punta Arenas): Amazon Web Services expanded its AWS Ground Station network to Punta Arenas to serve the South America (São Paulo) Region. This node enables satellite owners to connect with LEO satellites more frequently, significantly reducing the time between contacts for assets passing over the Southern Hemisphere.   

KSAT (Kongsberg Satellite Services) Antarctica Link: KSAT, a dominant player in commercial ground segments, operates a massive facility in Punta Arenas. Opened in 2019, the station consists of multiple antennas dedicated to Earth Observation data reception and gateway links for Iridium. KSAT markets this facility as part of a "unique pole-to-pole link" when combined with their Troll station in Antarctica, providing unparalleled capacity in the southern hemisphere. Furthermore, KSAT explicitly partners with Azure Orbital, providing the physical infrastructure that allows customers like Loft Orbital to test and onboard their missions seamlessly into the Microsoft cloud environment.   

SSC Space (Punta Arenas and Santiago): The Swedish Space Corporation (SSC) operates a sprawling 25-hectare satellite station in Punta Arenas, established in 2012, boasting independent power backup systems (UPS and diesel) to ensure uninterrupted operations. More recently, SSC installed a cutting-edge Optical Ground Station (OGS) at its site in Santiago, Chile. Supported by the European Space Agency (ESA) under the ScyLight program, this facility enables free-space laser communication with data rates up to 10 Gbit/s. Critically for U.S. defense infrastructure mapping, this OGS utilizes integrated modems supporting Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems (CCSDS) and Space Development Agency (SDA) communication standards.   

The integration of SDA-standard optical modems in commercial Chilean ground stations exposes the ultimate truth of the orchestration paradigm: the abstraction of Loft Orbital's ground segment ensures that highly classified U.S. Space Force and SDA experimental data collected by YAM satellites over the southern hemisphere is inherently touching civilian, foreign-owned terrestrial infrastructure before being encrypted and routed through commercial sovereign cloud boundaries back to the United States.

Contract Archaeology: STEP 2.0 and the SDA NExT Architecture
The deep integration of Loft Orbital into the U.S. defense architecture is cemented through massive contractual vehicles. In early 2023, Loft Federal—a subsidiary specifically created and FOCI-mitigated (Foreign Ownership, Control, or Influence) to handle national security space missions using only U.S. citizens—was established.   

In May 2025, Loft Federal was selected by the U.S. Space Force as one of 12 prime awardees for the Space Test Experiments Platform (STEP) 2.0 program. Administered by the DoD Space Test Program under Space Systems Command, STEP 2.0 is a 10-year, multiple-award Indefinite Delivery/Indefinite Quantity (IDIQ) contract with a ceiling value of $237 million. The contract empowers organizations to bid on task orders to supply small modular satellite platforms capable of integrating various military sensors and conducting agile, low-cost tests of emerging technologies in orbit. The first delivery order under this massive IDIQ is anticipated in January 2026.   

Simultaneously, Loft Federal is a critical subcontractor in the Space Development Agency's National Defense Space Architecture Experimental Testbed (NExT) program. Under a contract awarded in late 2022, Ball Aerospace (acting as the prime contractor) selected Loft Federal to perform spacecraft integration, procure commercial launch services, and ultimately operate a constellation of 10 experimental satellites on orbit utilizing the Cockpit software. Microsoft, continuing the orchestration triangle, provides the Azure Government cloud and ground station infrastructure for post-launch secure operations. This contractual web irrefutably confirms that Loft's "virtual mission" software—and by extension, its reliance on a global network of commercial South American downlinks—is fundamentally embedded as the operational backbone of the SDA's experimental architecture.   

While extensive supply chain disclosures exist for prime contractors, specific searches for Latin American manufacturing entities acting as subcontractors for the Loft "Hub" standard interface under these contracts yielded no verified positive results. The potential for ITAR/EAR export record trails via the Commerce Department BIS regarding Costa Rican or Brazilian hardware components within the YAM buses remains unverified and requires deeper forensic auditing of USASpending.gov tier-2 sub-awardee reports.

4. The Legacy of SICRAL and the Telespazio Corporate Veil
To comprehend the latent infrastructure of Latin American space networks, it is insufficient to solely examine modern cloud providers; one must investigate the deep historical and contemporary corporate veil of Telespazio. Telespazio operates as a massive European joint venture between the Italian defense and aerospace giant Leonardo (67%) and the French multinational Thales (33%).   

Post-WWII Lineage, the Vatican, and Italian Defense Partnerships
The Italian space program has maintained a continuous, highly integrated presence in global telecommunications, heavily entwined with both national defense objectives and the unique sovereign requirements of the Vatican City State. Historically, by the mid-1990s, the Vatican City State had established its own earth stations within the Vatican gardens, operating extensively with the Intelsat system to achieve global coverage stretching from Eastern Europe across the Atlantic.   

Concurrently, the Italian Ministry of Defense developed the highly classified SICRAL (Satellite Italiano Comunicazioni Riservate e Allarme) constellation. The SICRAL program, heavily supported by the industrial capabilities of Telespazio and Thales Alenia Space, provides critical strategic and tactical UHF and SHF military satellite communications. This infrastructure guarantees secure links for Italian terrestrial, naval, and air platforms and is seamlessly integrated with the infrastructure of NATO allied countries. Under the NATO SATCOM Post-2000 capability agreements, the SICRAL system formed a backbone of Alliance communications alongside French and British assets.   

Confidence Rating: GREEN. The historical lineage of Telespazio managing secure, extra-territorial military communications (SICRAL) and its historical interoperation with both state defense apparatuses and sovereign non-state entities (Vatican Intelsat nodes) is exhaustively documented in international defense literature, European space agency records, and NATO operational archives. This multi-decade legacy of operating highly secure infrastructure on foreign soil created the perfect administrative and technical blueprint for modern holding companies managing dual-use facilities.   

The Maricá Teleport and the Costa Rican Footprint
Telespazio has aggressively expanded its physical footprint across Latin America, adapting its legacy defense-contractor model into civilian commercial ventures that effectively serve dual-use capabilities. In Brazil, the subsidiary Telespazio Brasil has operated as a leading provider of satellite services since 1997. In early 2020, Telespazio announced a transformative partnership with the municipality of Maricá in the state of Rio de Janeiro, represented by the public development company Codemar (Companhia de Desenvolvimento de Maricá).   

Under this agreement, Codemar leases a massive parcel of land to Telespazio, which is responsible for designing and building what is marketed as one of the largest and most advanced Ground Segments for new satellite operators in Latin America. While publicly positioned to support the burgeoning Oil & Gas sector in the Bacia de Santos exploration field, the teleport is explicitly engineered to provide low-latency, very high-bandwidth services for emerging Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellite constellations alongside traditional Geostationary (GEO) assets.   

Furthermore, the structural integration of Italian defense interests into Brazilian infrastructure is solidified by a secondary joint venture: Leonardo&Codemar S.A.. This entity, owned 51% by Leonardo International and 49% by Codemar, is specifically focused on security, resilience, and critical infrastructure management. This creates an ideal corporate veil: an Italian defense prime controlling the majority stake in a local infrastructure firm, operating a massive LEO-capable teleport under the civilian guise of offshore oil monitoring.   

The Telespazio footprint extends deeply into Costa Rica as well. Corporate and regulatory records confirm that the legal entity "TELESPAZIO ARGENTINA S.A." (operating with the Costa Rican cédula jurídica 3-012-490070) holds an active presence and authorization within the country. SUTEL actas from 2025 demonstrate that Telespazio Argentina S.A. is actively engaged in the Costa Rican telecommunications market, possessing authorizations for the provision of internet access services and actively navigating the strict homologation requirements for telecommunications contracts mandated by the regulatory authority.   

Confidence Rating: AMBER. While Telespazio clearly holds legal entity status and active telecommunications authorizations in Costa Rica , the investigation sought to determine if the LeoLabs radar facility operated under Telespazio's specific ground station or ISP licenses to bypass regulatory hurdles. An exhaustive review of SUTEL resolutions confirms that LeoLabs Space Limitada operates under its own distinct regulatory filings and "permiso de uso de frecuencias" for radar usage. There is currently no documented administrative evidence within SUTEL archives suggesting that LeoLabs is operating as a franchise or sub-licensee under Telespazio's authorizations. However, the presence of both entities confirms that the Costa Rican jurisdiction is highly favorable for hosting parallel, advanced space-oriented infrastructure backed by foreign defense-adjacent primes.   

5. Defense Program Operational Synthesis: Tranche Correlation and Temporal Alignment
The transition of advanced space technologies from experimental agency programs to operational military architectures often leaves faint temporal signatures in the deployment of supporting ground infrastructure. A critical hypothesis of this investigation questioned whether the establishment of the LeoLabs Costa Rica site temporally correlated with DARPA Blackjack transition activities, suggesting the node was established to fill a specific experimental ground segment gap.

The DARPA Blackjack program was conceived to prove the military utility of proliferated LEO architectures, acting as a direct precursor to the SDA's Proliferated Warfighter Space Architecture (PWSA). A pivotal operational milestone for Blackjack occurred on June 30, 2021, with the launch of the Mandrake 2 spacecraft (comprising two satellites, Able and Baker) via the SpaceX Transporter-2 rideshare mission. Concurrently launched on this exact same vehicle was Loft Orbital's YAM-3 satellite, which, as previously established, hosted its own dedicated DARPA Blackjack demonstration payload.   

The primary mission of the Mandrake 2 spacecraft was to serve as an early risk-reduction flight for Blackjack, specifically tasked with proving out advanced, interoperable optical inter-satellite links (OISL) for a joint stakeholder team comprising DARPA, the SDA, and the Air Force Research Laboratory (AFRL). The ability to track, monitor, and quickly establish orbital parameters for these newly deployed, highly experimental assets immediately following separation from the launch vehicle is critical for mission success.

Confidence Rating: AMBER. There is a striking and highly significant temporal correlation between the physical deployment of the ground segment and the launch of the space segment. LeoLabs officially confirmed "fully operational" status for its Costa Rica S-band Space Radar on April 22, 2021. Exactly two months later, the critical DARPA Blackjack payloads (Mandrake 2 and YAM-3) were launched into orbit.   

While LeoLabs operates commercially as an SSA provider and is restricted by SUTEL from acting as an active TT&C command station , the sudden activation of a highly advanced, equatorial phased-array radar precisely eight weeks prior to a massive proliferated LEO military experiment launch strongly suggests the radar served a vital role. The Costa Rican node likely provided the critical early-orbit phase tracking, collision avoidance, and precise orbital situational awareness required for these experimental assets as they stabilized and began their OISL cross-link testing. However, adhering strictly to the ethical boundaries of this investigation, interpreting this precise temporal proximity as a directed, causational programmatic contract (i.e., DARPA directly funding the Costa Rica site's rapid construction) cannot be definitively verified without access to internal, classified SDA/DARPA task orders or unredacted financial disbursement logs, which remain outside the open-source domain.   

6. Conclusions and Strategic Implications
The exhaustive mapping of the latent infrastructure supporting U.S. and allied space architecture in Latin America yields several critical, strategically vital insights regarding the true nature of the "orchestration versus physicality" paradigm:

The Geopolitical Abstraction of Sovereignty: The heavy reliance on commercial software orchestration platforms—specifically Loft Orbital's Cockpit and Microsoft's Azure Orbital—successfully and intentionally insulates the Space Development Agency and DARPA from direct jurisdictional exposure in Latin America. When SDA NExT payloads operating on YAM satellites downlink sensitive data, they do not transmit to a branded "U.S. Space Force Base" in Chile. Instead, they utilize dynamic commercial leasebacks to push data through civilian-owned antennas operated by SSC Space, KSAT, or AWS Ground Station in Punta Arenas. This commercial orchestration entirely masks the actual control nodes of critical defense data, projecting U.S. space power across the Southern Hemisphere without the political friction or targetable footprint of sovereign military bases.   

Corporate Veils and the Evasion of Transparency: The Costa Rican regulatory framework provides a secure, reliable, yet highly opaque environment for commercial space operators. LeoLabs Space Limitada operates fully within the bounds of the law, regulated by SUTEL purely as a private, non-commercial radar facility. Crucially, the newly enacted Poder Generalísimo administrative workaround for the RTBF transparency registry allows foreign corporate officers to legally manage beneficial ownership declarations via local proxy without holding local digital signatures. This legal mechanism ensures that the deeper corporate capitalization layers and potential defense-funding origins of these commercial subsidiaries remain effectively shielded from immediate local public scrutiny.   

The Enduring, Evolving Influence of Telespazio: The historical legacy of the Italian space sector in Latin America is not a relic of the past; it is actively modernizing. By transitioning from the legacy GEO communications architecture of the SICRAL military era to managing massive, high-bandwidth LEO teleport infrastructure in Maricá, Brazil—under the guise of Codemar and Leonardo joint ventures—Telespazio represents a parallel, highly capable allied infrastructure backbone. Telespazio's continued legal authorizations in Costa Rica further demonstrate an intent to maintain a foothold in highly strategic equatorial and low-latitude spectrum environments.   

Verification of Sector Integrity (Negative Findings): The escalating concern regarding the potential capture or proxy-ownership of critical ground infrastructure by non-state actors or religious organizations was systematically disproven in this specific theater. Exhaustive corporate forensics utilizing the Costa Rican National Registry yield a definitive negative finding regarding the beneficial ownership, leaseback franchising, or direct infrastructure control of the LeoLabs segment by adjacent religious organizations, specifically the substantial LDS property holdings in the Guanacaste region. The infrastructure remains firmly, transparently (at the local subsidiary level), and legally in the hands of venture-backed commercial entities and traditional defense primes.   

Ultimately, the physical ground segment in Latin America is characterized by extreme fragmentation and deliberate legal compartmentalization. The orchestration model is not merely a convenient software architecture; it is a profound geopolitical strategy. By breaking the traditional, linear link between a military satellite and a military ground station, defense agencies have created a global, resilient, and legally nebulous data collection apparatus that seamlessly blends into the background radiation of international commercial telecommunications.
