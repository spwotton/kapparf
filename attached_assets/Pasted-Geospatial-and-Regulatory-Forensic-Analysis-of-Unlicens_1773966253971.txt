Utilizing advanced regulatory cross-referencing against the Costa Rican Superintendency of Telecommunications (SUTEL), the Ministry of Science, Innovation, Technology and Telecommunications (MICITT), municipal land-use registries, and historical corporate data, this document evaluates multiple operational hypotheses. The decision to place three substantial radio towers in a valley, rather than on a high-altitude peak, is the single most important technical clue regarding the physical and operational nature of the site.Geography and Hydrology of San Isidro and TacacoríThe district of San Isidro, located in the broader canton of Alajuela, is characterized by undulating terrain, profound vegetation, and highly localized river systems that drain from the higher elevations. The broader Alajuela canton encompasses varied ecosystems ranging from the volcanic slopes of the Poás Volcano down to the flatter central plains, intersected by waterways such as the Río Catarata, Quebrada La Saca, and the falls of the Río La Paz. The specific settlement of Tacacorí rests in a localized topographical depression, anchored historically and socially by the Catholic Church—a substantial structure built in 1934 on land acquired from local landowner Julio Ugalde.This specific valley environment is characterized by distinct soil properties. Valleys in the Central Valley region of Costa Rica, particularly setups proximate to river systems and subterranean aquifers, generally exhibit much higher soil moisture content than the rocky, well-drained volcanic slopes above them. This geological and hydrological reality is paramount to telecommunications engineering, specifically regarding ground conductivity.The Physics of Three-Tower Directional ArraysIn telecommunications engineering, a multi-tower configuration located in a valley is almost exclusively indicative of an Amplitude Modulation (AM) directional broadcast array. Modern cellular communications, Frequency Modulation (FM) radio, and ultra-high frequency (UHF) television signals rely on line-of-sight propagation. For these technologies, height is the most critical variable; engineers place antennas on mountain peaks or tall buildings to maximize the distance to the visual horizon. Placing an FM or cellular tower in a deep valley would severely restrict its coverage area, effectively trapping the signal within the surrounding hillsides.Conversely, AM radio signals, operating in the medium wave band (typically 525 kHz to 1705 kHz), propagate primarily via ground waves. Ground-wave propagation is highly dependent on the electrical conductivity of the earth's surface. High soil moisture, such as that found in river valleys like Tacacorí, offers superior ground conductivity, drastically reducing signal attenuation and enhancing the efficiency and reach of the AM signal over vast distances. Therefore, from an engineering perspective, a valley is the optimal—and often necessary—location for a high-power AM transmission facility.Furthermore, the presence of exactly three towers is highly diagnostic. A single AM tower radiates electromagnetic energy omnidirectionally, creating a circular coverage pattern. However, to prevent electromagnetic interference with other regional or international stations operating on adjacent frequencies, or to focus the broadcast energy efficiently over heavily populated areas (such as the San José metropolitan sector) while avoiding unpopulated mountainous zones, broadcast engineers utilize multiple towers to create a phased directional array.By feeding the radio frequency signal into three carefully spaced towers at specific electrical phases and power amplitudes, the resulting electromagnetic wave forms a precise, non-circular geometric pattern. The signals from the three towers constructively and destructively interfere with one another, amplifying the signal in desired directions and creating nulls (zones of zero transmission) in others. Therefore, the architectural reality of three towers in the Tacacorí valley definitively points toward a legacy, high-power AM broadcasting facility, effectively ruling out modern localized cellular nodes, small-scale amateur radio setups, or localized point-to-point microwave links.The National Regulatory Ecosystem: MICITT, SUTEL, and Municipal GovernanceThe inability to locate current, active licenses for these three towers in public registries is a symptom of a highly complex, and currently disrupted, regulatory ecosystem in Costa Rica. Telecommunications infrastructure is governed by a strict legal framework managed primarily by two state entities: the Ministry of Science, Innovation, Technology and Telecommunications (MICITT) as the sector rector, and the Superintendency of Telecommunications (SUTEL) as the technical regulator.The Architecture of Telecommunications ConcessionsUnder the regulations stemming from the Costa Rican General Telecommunications Law—specifically Articles 17 through 20, 34 through 36, 45 through 48, and 134—the acquisition and maintenance of a radio frequency requires a rigorous, multi-staged approval process. An entity must first submit a formalized request to MICITT using established physical and digital protocols. Once MICITT receives the application, it forwards the dossier to SUTEL for technical evaluation, a process governed by recent resolutions such as RCS-043-2026.SUTEL evaluates the request based on stringent technical parameters. To facilitate this, SUTEL utilizes a dedicated web platform where applicants must upload detailed, formatted Excel spreadsheets outlining exact transmission parameters, geographical coordinates, and engineering specifications. This system is designed to catalog every single active transmitter in the country, tracking everything from fixed radio links to complex satellite earth stations. SUTEL maintains public-facing digital visors, such as the spectrum visor and the infrastructure visor, allowing citizens to view registered operators, coverage maps, and broadband availability. The fact that the Tacacorí towers do not appear in these modernized digital visors requires a deeper investigation into systemic regulatory failures or legal loopholes.Municipal Zoning and Infrastructural MandatesAt the local level, the canton of Alajuela and the specific district of San Isidro enforce their own stringent infrastructural zoning laws, heavily influenced by the mandates of the National Telecommunications Infrastructure Action Plan (PAIT 2022-2023). Municipal regulations explicitly mandate the georeferencing of all telecommunications assets using precise coordinates, strictly requiring latitude and longitude to be recorded in modern formats such as WGS84 or the localized CRTM05 projection.Furthermore, these municipal guidelines dictate spatial limitations, such as mandatory minimum distances ranging from 80 meters to 500 meters between competing telecommunications infrastructures. The San Isidro municipal council has actively formalized these infrastructural liaisons and enforcement mechanisms, as evidenced by official directives such as the decree "Oficio MSIH-CM-SCM-464-2022" executed in July 2022. Therefore, any legal tower built in recent years would be heavily documented in both national SUTEL databases and localized municipal geodatabases. This judicial intervention effectively froze the entire broadcasting regulatory landscape, creating massive blind spots in public registries.The Suspension of Public TendersPrior to late 2025, SUTEL had established a rigid, modernized timeline for auctioning and renewing radio and television frequencies. This process was designed to clear up historical anomalies and transition all legacy broadcasters onto newly formalized, digitally tracked concessions. The planned schedule was highly structured, involving public publication of economic offers within one business day, subsequent phases for additional bidding (puja), and a mandatory five-day analysis period for evaluating the economic proposals.However, this entire modernization effort was abruptly halted. On November 26, 2025, the Constitutional Court ordered the total precautionary suspension of the public tender procedures. The court mandated this freeze while it analyzed the foundational merits of a legal appeal (recurso de amparo) filed against the public contests promoted for the assignment of these frequencies. Following this definitive sentence, SUTEL announced that the competitive process would not continue under the originally proposed terms, and MICITT was instructed to completely restart the procedural design, taking into account the court's constitutional observations.Impact on Legacy Broadcasters and SUTEL DatabasesThe scope of this judicial suspension is vast. It explicitly froze the assignment and renewal processes across the most critical broadcasting bands:Radio AM: The segment encompassing 525 kHz to 1705 kHz.Radio FM: The segment encompassing 88 MHz to 108 MHz.Open Television: The VHF segment (174 MHz to 216 MHz) and the UHF segment (470 MHz to 608 MHz).While frequencies assigned by law to state institutions—such as the University of Costa Rica, the State Distance University, and the National System of Radio and Television—were explicitly excluded from the process and allowed to continue operating normally , private, commercial, and religious broadcasters were thrust into a state of profound regulatory stasis.For a legacy AM broadcaster operating a three-tower directional array in a place like Tacacorí, this suspension means they are likely operating under an extended, grandfathered permissibility. Because their new licenses could not be finalized, processed, or digitally registered due to the court order, their updated infrastructural data remains locked within suspended administrative dockets rather than being published on SUTEL's modern digital visors. The agency cited extreme volatility in the national exchange rate as the primary catalyst for this massive reduction in deployable resources.
