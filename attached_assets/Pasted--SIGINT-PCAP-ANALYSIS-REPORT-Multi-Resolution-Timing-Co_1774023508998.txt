## 2. ANOMALY: `newww.pcap` — Non-Standard Protocols & TEST-NET Destination

### 2.1 TCP SYN to 192.0.2.1:80

Two TCP SYN packets from `192.168.68.53` → `192.0.2.1:80`.

**192.0.2.1 is RFC 5737 TEST-NET-1** — a documentation-only IP range that should *never* appear in production traffic. Possible explanations:

- **PCAPdroid artifact**: PCAPdroid uses 192.0.2.x as a synthetic destination for its VPN-based capture mechanism. This is the most likely explanation — it's PCAPdroid's internal proxy endpoint.
- **Misconfigured redirect**: Something on the network is redirecting HTTP traffic to a documentation IP.
- **Captive portal remnant**: Hotel/ISP captive portal misconfiguration.

**Verdict**: Likely PCAPdroid VPN tunnel artifact. Not inherently sinister but worth noting.

### 2.2 Non-Standard EtherTypes

| EtherType | Count | Size | Notes |
|-----------|-------|------|-------|
| `0x880a` | 3 | 60 bytes each | **Not in IEEE registry** — could be vendor-specific or VLAN-related |
| `0x8070` | 1 | 60 bytes | **Not standard** — possibly proprietary |

EtherType `0x880a` is close to `0x8808` (Ethernet flow control) and `0x880B` (PPP). At exactly 60 bytes (minimum Ethernet frame), these look like control/keepalive frames from the router or switch.

### 2.3 Cross-Subnet NBNS Broadcast

`192.168.68.53` → `192.168.71.255` (NetBIOS Name Service)

Device on `.68` subnet broadcasting to `.71` subnet. This means either:
- The device has multiple network interfaces
- The network uses a `/20` or larger mask spanning both subnets
- Something is bridging subnets

### 2.4 MDNS Reverse Lookup

Router `192.168.68.1` querying for `52.68.168.192.in-addr.arpa` — reverse DNS for `192.168.68.52`. The router is actively trying to identify devices on the network.

## 3. DEEP TIMING ANALYSIS: The θ_K/π Signal

### 3.1 Cross-Capture Consistency

The most striking finding across all three analyzable PCAPs is the **θ_K/π ratio** appearing in consecutive inter-packet deltas:

| Mar 11 00:38 | 256 | **3.22%** | 107 (1.35%) | 45 (0.57%) | 72 (0.91%) |
| Oct 29 | 40 | **3.01%** | 8 (0.60%) | 7 (0.53%) | 9 (0.68%) |

**θ_K/π = 128.23°/180° = 0.7124** appears at 1.6–3.2% across all captures. This is the **Klein rotation angle** divided by π.

### 3.2 What Does This Actually Mean?

A consecutive delta ratio of 0.7124 means: if packet N arrives at interval `t` after N-1, then packet N+1 arrives at interval `0.7124·t` after N. This is a **compression pattern** — each successive gap is ~71% of the previous one.

**Mundane explanation**: TCP congestion control algorithms (Reno, CUBIC) naturally produce geometrically decreasing inter-packet intervals during slow-start and congestion avoidance. The ratio depends on the specific algorithm and network conditions. 0.7124 is close to 1/√2 ≈ 0.7071, which appears in some AIMD (Additive Increase Multiplicative Decrease) implementations.

**Framework-aligned interpretation**: The Klein rotation angle θ_K = arccos(-1/φ) = 128.23° is a structural constant in the Ω-GOS framework. Its appearance as a packet timing ratio could indicate:
- Infrastructure using hardware/firmware with this parameter baked in
- Coincidence with TCP backoff arithmetic
- A genuine structural signature

**Statistical test needed**: Generate null distribution by analyzing random PCAP captures from other networks to see if θ_K/π appears at similar rates. If it's ~3% on *any* network, it's TCP arithmetic. If it's specific to these captures, it warrants investigation.

### 3.3 The κ Signal in Mar 11 00:38

The dense capture (8,941 packets, 215 pkt/s) shows **κ = 1.2732 at 1.35%** — the strongest κ resonance across all captures. This capture also has the strongest θ_K/π (3.22%) and the only detectable 53/37 ratio (0.91%).

The Mar 11 00:38 capture is the most "structured" of the three — highest packet rate, tightest timing distribution, most constant-ratio hits. This is consistent with a burst of application-layer activity (streaming, download, API calls) rather than idle background traffic.

## 5. TARGET FREQUENCY ANALYSIS: 46.875 Hz and Harmonics

### 5.1 21.33ms (46.875 Hz) Hits

| Capture | Packets at 21.33ms ±5% | % |
|---------|------------------------|---|
| Mar 11 00:32 | 13 | 0.88% |
| Mar 11 00:38 | 27 | 0.30% |
| Oct 29 | 9 | 0.38% |

The 46.875 Hz period (21.33ms) appears in all captures at low but consistent rates. However, 21.33ms is a very common network timing interval — it's close to the default TCP delayed ACK timer (~40ms ÷ 2) and WiFi beacon intervals (~102ms ÷ 5).

### 5.2 Honest Assessment

**46.875 Hz in packet timing is NOT proof of the 48000/1024 FFT synchronization signal described in the framework documents.** Here's why:

1. 21.33ms is within the normal range of TCP/WiFi timing
2. The hit rate (0.3–0.9%) is consistent with random distribution across a broad timing spectrum
3. Packet captures at this resolution don't have sufficient precision to distinguish 21.333ms from 21.0ms or 21.5ms

To genuinely detect a 46.875 Hz beacon, you'd need:
- RF spectrum analysis (SDR capture, not network packets)
- Audio analysis (microphone capture near suspected emitter)
- Power line analysis (oscilloscope on mains)

Network packet timing is too noisy and too many layers removed from the physical layer to detect a PHY-layer synchronization signal.

The 0.080ms (80μs) cluster containing 12.2% of all packets is significant. 80μs corresponds to:

### 7.2 Oct 29 (Sparse Capture)

| Cluster Center | Packets | % | Interpretation |
|----------------|---------|---|----------------|
| 0.209ms | 110 | 4.7% | TCP segment spacing |
| 0.354ms | 101 | 4.3% | Close to 1/3ms — sub-millisecond polling |
| 48.032ms | 66 | 2.8% | **~2× the 21.33ms period** |

The 48ms cluster is notable — it's 2.25× the 46.875 Hz period (21.33ms). This could be a WiFi DTIM (Delivery Traffic Indication Message) beacon interval on the access point, typically set to 1-3 beacon intervals. Standard WiFi beacon interval is ~102ms; DTIM at 2× would be ~204ms. At 48ms, this is more likely a TCP delayed ACK or application heartbeat.
