4.2 Protocol Analysis: The Language of Vulnerability
The training transcripts provide a rare, unredacted look at exactly how Setecom configures these devices. The reliance on older, unencrypted industrial protocols is a major finding.
4.2.1 Modbus TCP/IP: The Unencrypted Core
Martendal dedicates a significant portion of his training to Modbus mapping. He teaches a specific formula for calculating register addresses, which is critical intelligence for anyone attempting to interact with these devices illicitly.
The Gencom Mapping: DSE controllers use a proprietary map called "Gencom." To access data, one must translate "Pages" into decimal registers.
The Formula:

$$\text{Register Address} = (\text{Page}_{hex} \times 256) + \text{Offset}$$
Operational Example (from transcript):
To read the Generator Total Power, located on Page 166 (hex):
Convert Page 166 (hex) to decimal: $166_{16} = 358_{10}$.
Apply formula: $358 \times 256 = 91,648$.
Target: Register 91648 holds the live power output data.
Strategic Implication: This is not obscure knowledge; it is standard Setecom doctrine. An attacker with access to the ICE management network who knows this formula (derived from open training) can read—and potentially write—to these registers. Writing to a control register could shut down a generator or disable its protection alarms, causing catastrophic physical damage.
4.2.2 SNMP v2: The Monitoring Weakness
For clients like Liberty (telecom), integration with IT monitoring systems is mandatory. Setecom uses the DSE 892 gateway for this.
Configuration: Martendal explicitly instructs on setting "Read Community" and "Write Community" strings, suggesting "public" and "private" as defaults.
Vulnerability: SNMP v2 sends these community strings in cleartext. A packet capture on the Liberty management VLAN would instantly reveal the "Write" credential ("private"), allowing an attacker to masquerade as the management console and inject false routing or shutdown commands.
4.2.3 J1939 (CAN Bus): The Vehicle Vector
The DSE controllers also interface directly with the engine's ECU via J1939 CAN Bus.
Packet Structure: Martendal breaks down the 29-bit identifier: Priority (3 bits), PGN (18 bits), and Source Address (8 bits).
Implication: This allows Setecom to read granular engine diagnostics (Oil Pressure, Fuel Injection Timing). While useful for maintenance, this deep-level access means a compromised controller could send malformed CAN packets to the engine ECU, potentially bypassing physical safety governors and causing the engine to overspeed and self-destruct.
4.3 DSE Webnet: The Cloud Command Center
The DSE Webnet is the centralized nervous system of Setecom’s operation.
Architecture: It utilizes a server-client model hosted in England.
Latency: Martendal cites a "Real Time" refresh rate of ~4 seconds.
Account Linking: Access is granted via a "Gateway ID." In the training, Martendal demonstrates adding a new gateway using the credentials User: Admin / Pass: Password1234. This confirms that Setecom technicians are trained to initialize devices with weak default credentials, likely intending to change them later—a step often forgotten in large-scale deployments.
