UNC2814 (Gallium) and the GRIDTIDE Command Architecture
In March 2026, the Costa Rican government publicly announced that the Costa Rican Electricity Institute (ICE) had suffered a severe security breach linked to foreign cyber espionage. The incident resulted in the unauthorized extraction of approximately nine gigabytes of sensitive internal email data from locally hosted servers. The intrusion was detected in late January 2026 following critical intelligence provided by the Google Threat Intelligence Group (GTIG) and Mandiant. The diplomatic fallout from this event was immediate, with the Chinese embassy publicly rejecting the accusations and demanding technical evidence from Costa Rican authorities, thereby elevating the incident to a matter of acute national security and international geopolitical friction.
The cornerstone of the 2026 ICE breach, and the broader global espionage campaign orchestrated by UNC2814, was the deployment of a novel, C-based backdoor designated as GRIDTIDE. The GRIDTIDE malware represents a masterclass in modern network evasion, completely eschewing the use of traditional command-and-control (C2) infrastructure—such as hardcoded IP addresses or dynamically generated domains—in favor of abusing the legitimate application programming interface (API) of Google Sheets. By embedding its malicious traffic within the high-volume, globally trusted streams of encrypted cloud communications, GRIDTIDE renders standard network intrusion detection systems and edge firewalls virtually obsolete. Security appliances inherently trust traffic directed to major software-as-a-service (SaaS) providers, allowing the threat actors to disguise their C2 beacons as benign enterprise activity.
The operational mechanics of the GRIDTIDE payload are meticulously engineered for high availability, fault tolerance, and absolute low observability. Upon execution, the malware authenticates to the Google Cloud platform utilizing compromised or embedded service account credentials. The payload requires a specific 16-byte cryptographic key, which is intentionally stored locally in a separate file on the compromised host, to decrypt its configuration parameters using Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) with a 128-bit key in Cipher Block Chaining (CBC) mode. Once the configuration is decrypted and active, GRIDTIDE treats a specifically designated Google Spreadsheet not as a document for viewing, but as a synchronous, highly structured database for tasking and data transfer.
The malware employs a discrete cell-based polling mechanism to facilitate its operations. Cell A1 functions as the primary command register; the malware polls this specific cell via the Google Sheets API. If an instruction is present, the malware parses the command, executes it within the host environment, and subsequently overwrites Cell A1 with a status code, such as S-C-R to indicate a successful execution by the server component. If Cell A1 is empty, the malware initiates a highly variable sleep cycle. It initially polls every second, but after a designated number of empty returns, it introduces a randomized backoff interval between five and ten minutes to evade behavioral heuristics and rate-limiting protocols. Cells A2 through An serve as the primary data transfer channels, facilitating the upload of exfiltrated data or the download of secondary payloads, rigidly segmented into 45-kilobyte fragments to conform to API constraints. Finally, Cell V1 is strictly designated for host fingerprinting, storing exfiltrated environmental parameters such as the endpoint hostname, local IP configuration, operating system version, and active user profile.
The initial access vectors for UNC2814 remain tactically fluid and difficult to pinpoint due to their longevity, though historical operations heavily favor the initial exploitation of vulnerable edge devices and public-facing web servers. Once initial access is achieved, the actors rely heavily on "living-off-the-land" (LotL) techniques to maintain stealth. For example, the compiled GRIDTIDE payload is frequently renamed to xapt to masquerade as the legitimate Advanced Packaging Tool utility in Debian-based architectures, allowing it to blend seamlessly into routine process lists. Persistence is securely established by embedding the executable within a systemd service file, ensuring the malware autonomously respawns upon system reboot. Lateral movement is largely facilitated through the hijacking of secure shell (SSH) service accounts, avoiding the need for noisy exploitation within the internal network. Deep Sea Electronics (DSE) Gateway Compromises
Deep Sea Electronics (DSE) manufactures highly specialized, industry-standard control modules utilized globally for critical operations such as generator synchronization, mains load sharing, and localized power grid management. Devices such as the DSE855 and DSE892 serve as critical ethernet communications gateways, fundamentally bridging internal USB or RS485 serial networks with external Local Area Networks (LAN) or wide-reaching internet connections. These gateways are designed to facilitate remote monitoring and the direct manipulation of heavy machinery via the Modbus TCP and Simple Network Management Protocol (SNMP) protocols, typically operating over standardized ports.
In mid-2024, the Zero Day Initiative (ZDI) and the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) published multiple advisories regarding critical vulnerabilities discovered within the DSE855 firmware (specifically version 1.1.0 and earlier), posing severe, immediate risks to operational technology (OT) environments globally. The most critical of these disclosures is CVE-2024-5947, categorized as a Missing Authentication for Critical Function vulnerability (CWE-306). This architectural flaw permits a network-adjacent, unauthenticated attacker to execute a direct object reference via a simple HTTP GET request to the /Backup.bin endpoint on the device's web server. This binary file stores the complete configuration of the device in plaintext, including highly sensitive administrative credentials, supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA) passwords, and detailed network topology data.
The seamless extraction of these credentials allows malicious actors to completely bypass all authentication mechanisms, granting them full supervisory control over the connected DSE generator controller. The subsequent deployment of a secondary vulnerability, CVE-2024-5950—a stack-based buffer overflow stemming from the improper validation of multipart form variables—grants the attacker arbitrary remote code execution (RCE) within the deeply privileged context of the device's NXP LPC4357JBD208 microcontroller. Furthermore, vulnerabilities such as CVE-2024-5949 and CVE-2024-5952 expose unauthenticated logic errors within the handling of multipart boundaries and restart handlers. These flaws can be exploited to force infinite loops or execute arbitrary device restarts, creating immediate denial-of-service (DoS) conditions. In a practical scenario, an attacker could selectively sever remote monitoring and control capabilities precisely during a critical power fluctuation or load-shedding event, effectively blinding grid operators.
