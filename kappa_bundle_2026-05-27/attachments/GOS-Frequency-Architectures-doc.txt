High-Frequency Signal Constraints and the Nyquist Boundary
Deploying acoustic or electromagnetic carriers at or near $60,000\text{ Hz}$ involves navigating distinct biological and physical constraints. Biologically, while the human auditory system is limited to an upper frequency threshold of approximately $20\text{ kHz}$, other mammalian species exhibit heightened sensitivity at $60\text{ kHz}$.12 In canines, the audible range extends from $40\text{ Hz}$ to $60,000\text{ Hz}$ ($60\text{ kHz}$), with their peak sensitivity band situated between $4\text{ kHz}$ and $10\text{ kHz}$.15 The canine outer ear's funnel-like geometry and the increased density of sensory cells within their cochlea facilitate the capture and amplification of these high-frequency signals.16
Physically, high-frequency signals attenuate rapidly over distance due to atmospheric absorption, molecular relaxation of oxygen and nitrogen, and local humidity barriers.17 While this attenuation limits the range of a $60\text{ kHz}$ signal, it serves as an effective security mechanism for localized, high-directionality communication, preventing signal intercept by distant observers.17
From a digital capture perspective, standard signal processing systems operating at an audio sampling rate ($S_r$) of $48,000\text{ Hz}$ face an absolute physical limit known as the Nyquist boundary.5 The Nyquist limit ($f_{\text{Nyquist}}$) is defined as:

$$f_{\text{Nyquist}} = \frac{S_r}{2} = 24,000\text{ Hz}$$
Any signal component at or above $60,000\text{ Hz}$ cannot be digitally resolved by a standard $48\text{ kHz}$ system and will instead fold back (alias) into the audible spectrum, causing severe digital distortion.5 To digitally capture, process, or demodulate a $60\text{ kHz}$ carrier without aliasing, a software-defined radio (SDR) or high-speed acoustic acquisition system must utilize a hardware sampling rate of at least twice the target frequency 5:

$$S_r \ge 2 \times 60,000\text{ Hz} = 120,000\text{ Hz}\text{ (120 kHz)}$$
If processing is limited to standard $48\text{ kHz}$ pipelines, analysts must employ multi-resolution FFT structures (scaling up to $N = 65,536$ samples to achieve an ultra-fine spectral bin resolution of $0.732\text{ Hz}$) to isolate subtle hardware drift and low-frequency telemetry modulation, while relying on dedicated RF front-ends to step down the high-frequency carriers.5
