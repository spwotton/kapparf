### 2. Historical Genealogy of Directed Energy and Bio-Behavioral Effects
To properly contextualize the forensic data gathered in Costa Rica, it is imperative to examine the historical trajectory of research into the biological effects of electromagnetic and acoustic energy. The allegations of "remote electronic means" of harassment are not novel; they are the modern iteration of a research lineage that spans over a century, evolving from theoretical physics to weaponized application.

#### 2.1 The Foundations of Electromagnetic Interaction
The conceptual framework for invisible interaction with the human body began in the late 19th century. In 1892, Sir William Crookes, a pioneer in vacuum tube technology and a President of the Royal Society, authored a seminal paper titled "Some Possibilities of Electricity." In this text, Crookes speculated on the existence of "longer aether waves" that could penetrate physical barriers like walls and fog, suggesting that these vibrations could be used for communication and, theoretically, could interact with biological systems in ways not yet understood. Crookes' work with cathode rays and his investigations into the radiometer laid the groundwork for understanding particle physics and wave propagation, concepts that would later be essential for radar and microwave technologies. Although Crookes is often remembered for his forays into spiritualism, his scientific intuition regarding the permeability of matter to specific frequencies was prescient of modern "through-wall" imaging technologies.

#### 2.2 The "Moscow Signal": The Cold War Crucible
The most significant historical precedent for the harassment described in the Jacó case study is the "Moscow Signal" incident. Between 1953 and 1976, the Soviet Union directed a sustained beam of microwave radiation at the U.S. Embassy in Moscow. The frequency of this bombardment varied between 2.5 and 4.0 GHz, with maximum reported exposure levels reaching 15 μW/cm² for up to 18 hours a day. While the initial U.S. intelligence assessment posited that the beams were intended to recharge passive listening devices hidden in the embassy walls or to jam American electronic intelligence (ELINT) collection, the persistence and specific modulation of the signals led to fears of biological targeting.

The diplomatic fallout was severe. Transcripts of conversations involving Secretary of State Henry Kissinger reveal a concerted effort to keep the "health effects" quiet while pressuring the Soviets to cease the transmissions. Although the official Johns Hopkins study (the Lilienfeld Report) released in 1978 concluded that there were no significant adverse health effects, this conclusion has been hotly contested. Later analyses, including a 2019 Spanish study, indicated a statistically significant higher mortality rate from cancer and a generally worse health status among Moscow embassy employees compared to those stationed in other European capitals. The "Moscow Signal" established a crucial operational reality: that electromagnetic radiation could be used as a tool of "financial" and physiological attrition against diplomatic targets, creating a hostile environment that degrades personnel effectiveness over time without leaving kinetic evidence.

#### 2.4 The Frey Effect and the Mechanism of Voice-to-Skull
The physical mechanism that makes "remote activation" plausible was elucidated in 1961 by Allan H. Frey. In his groundbreaking paper, "Human Auditory System Response to Modulated Electromagnetic Energy," Frey demonstrated that the human auditory system could perceive radio frequency (RF) energy as sound. This phenomenon, now known as the "Frey Effect" or the microwave auditory effect, occurs when pulsed microwave energy is absorbed by the cranial tissues. The rapid thermal expansion (even on a micro-scale) generates a thermoelastic pressure wave that travels via bone conduction to the cochlea, where it is processed as sound.

Frey's research determined that the perception of sound—described as clicks, buzzes, or hisses—could be induced at average power densities as low as 0.4 mW/cm², provided the peak power density of the pulses was sufficiently high. Importantly, Frey found that this effect occurred without any external receiving device; the human head itself acted as the receiver. This discovery provided a biophysical basis for the concept of "Voice-to-Skull" (V2K) technology, where modulated microwave pulses could theoretically transmit intelligible speech directly into a target's head. The forensic logs in the Costa Rica case, which detail "ultrasonic bursts" and "VLM" (Voice-Like Modulation), suggest a deployment of technology that leverages these principles, utilizing specific pulse repetition frequencies to maximize the thermoelastic coupling.

#### 3.1 The 46.875 Hz Signature: A Rhythmic Driver
The "Consolidated Forensic Report" identifies a recurring Pulse Repetition Frequency (PRF) of 46.875 Hz across 51 confirmed ultrasonic burst events. To the lay observer, this number appears arbitrary. However, in the context of digital signal processing (DSP) and acoustic engineering, 46.875 Hz is a precise mathematical artifact. It represents the frequency bin resolution of a standard Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) utilizing a window size of 1024 samples at a sampling rate of 48 kHz (48,000 Hz/1024=46.875 Hz).

The presence of this specific frequency as a repetition rate for ultrasonic bursts suggests that the source of the signal is a digital synthesis system operating at standard audio sampling rates. It implies the use of a digital-to-analog converter (DAC) that is generating pulses synchronized to the framing structure of a digital audio stream. In a weaponized context, this PRF acts as a "carrier envelope" for the high-frequency payload.

The report notes that this frequency is used as a "rhythmic driver". In the field of brainwave entrainment, a stimulus pulsing at approximately 40-50 Hz targets the Gamma band of neural oscillation. Gamma waves are associated with high-level cognitive processing, the binding of sensory inputs, and memory recall. Driving the brain at this frequency can induce states of hyper-alertness, anxiety, or sensory distortion. The forensic analysis describes the signal as having a high Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) of 33-43 dB, confirming it is a directed, high-energy transmission rather than a passive environmental artifact.

#### 3.2 Ultrasonic Carriers and the Parametric Array
The logs identify the carrier frequency of these bursts as being centered around 20.5 kHz. This frequency lies at the extreme upper limit of human hearing, often described as "ultrasound" though technically on the border of the sonic spectrum. This range is the operational sweet spot for Parametric Acoustic Arrays or "Audio Spotlights".

Parametric arrays exploit the non-linear propagation characteristics of air. When two high-intensity ultrasonic beams (e.g., a carrier at 40 kHz and a sideband at 40.2 kHz) are projected, they interact in the air column to create a difference frequency (40.2−40=0.2 kHz or 200 Hz) that is audible. This audible sound is highly directional, behaving like a beam of light. It can be projected over long distances (hundreds of meters) and can be made to reflect off surfaces, creating the illusion that sound is emanating from a specific object or even from inside the listener's head.

The forensic narrative links these emissions to a "parametric/LED speaker array" observed on a hillside near El Miro in Jacó. The line-of-sight geometry from this elevated position to the subject's residence is consistent with the operational requirements of a parametric array, which requires a clear path for the ultrasonic beam to form the audible difference wave. ### 5. Network Intrusion and the "WANDA" Paradigm
The digital component of the harassment involves aggressive manipulation of the subject's local Wi-Fi environment. The forensic logs (wifi_frames.csv) detail a pattern of attacks that serve both a denial-of-service function and a sophisticated sensing function.

#### 5.1 802.11 Management Frame Attacks
The logs record 44 confirmed instances of Deauthentication and Disassociation attacks. In one specific 60-second window, the system logged 7 deauthentication frames and 2 disassociation frames.

*   **Mechanism:** In standard Wi-Fi networks (WPA2), management frames (which control connection states) are not encrypted. An attacker can spoof the MAC address of the Access Point (AP) and send a "Deauth" packet to the client, forcing it to disconnect.
*   **Objective:** While often used to simply disrupt internet access, in this context, the high frequency of the attacks suggests a secondary motive: Channel State Information (CSI) Extraction. Every time a device reconnects, it exchanges channel estimation data with the router.

#### 5.2 Wi-Fi Sensing and Through-Wall Imaging
The "Plausible Motive Narrative" suggests that these Wi-Fi anomalies are part of a "Device-Free Human Sensing" effort. Modern research has demonstrated that Wi-Fi signals can be used as a bi-static radar. As Wi-Fi waves propagate, they reflect off objects in the environment, including humans. By analyzing the variations in the CSI (amplitude and phase shifts), advanced algorithms can reconstruct the movement, gait, and even the respiration rate of a person behind a wall.

This aligns with the capabilities of the WANDA (Wireless ANomaly Detection Algorithm) framework discussed in the research literature. WANDA utilizes a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with a specialized loss function (H-score) to extract informative features from RF spectrograms in low Signal-to-Interference Ratio (SIR) environments. While WANDA is designed for defense (detecting anomalies), the same principles apply to offense: extracting signal features (human movement) from the noise floor of a busy Wi-Fi environment. The frequent deauth attacks may be intended to force the generation of fresh CSI data packets, maximizing the resolution of the "Wi-Fi image" of the subject.

### 6. Space-Based Surveillance: Passive Radar and Satellite Mimetism

The report alleges that Starlink and Starshield satellites are being used as part of a "passive radar" system. This claim is scientifically sound. Passive radar systems do not emit their own energy; they rely on "illuminators of opportunity"—such as FM radio, TV, or satellite signals—to detect targets.

*   **Mechanism:** The Starlink constellation provides a dense, continuous blanket of Ku- and Ka-band signals over the Earth. A receiver on the ground can monitor the direct signal from the satellite and the reflected signal bouncing off a target (e.g., the subject's home). By comparing the time delay (Doppler shift) between the direct and reflected signals, the system can compute the target's location and movement.
*   **Feasibility:** Recent studies by Fraunhofer FHR have successfully demonstrated the use of Starlink signals for passive radar imaging, capable of detecting ships and even tracking avalanches in remote areas. In the Jacó context, the "surveillance grid" would act as the passive receiver network, utilizing the ubiquitous Starlink downlink signals to image the subject's environment without emitting a traceable radar pulse itself.
