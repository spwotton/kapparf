# THE FREQUENCY
*A Satirical Overview — Names Changed for Audio Distribution*

---

There is a small beach town on the Pacific coast of a Central American republic that functions, on the surface, as every Pacific beach town does: surf schools, open-air restaurants, expatriates sitting on plastic chairs drinking local beer, the smell of salt and diesel and something fried. Call it Puerto Palma. It will do.

Sam arrived in Puerto Palma the way people arrive at places that change them — incrementally. One month in 2021. Three months in 2022. Six months in 2023, renting a house from a German national named Wolfgang Hess, who ran a hospitality operation called Shangri La on the main road and whose connections in town were extensive and peculiar. Then January 2024, and Sam stopped leaving.

He had gotten sober in 2012 — heroin, the usual catastrophe — and moved into a halfway house in the northern United States run by a man named Skip Murphy. The house manager was a young man named Craig Navarro. Craig was pleasant, organized, and ambitious. He would go on to work for a major municipal software company at a reported salary of eight hundred thousand dollars a year before being recruited by one of the largest technology companies in the world to sell artificial intelligence products to government agencies. He now operates out of a town in New York State in the same regional corridor as a contact of Sam's who works for a federal law enforcement and intelligence agency. This is noted as proximity. Nothing more.

Sam's sponsor in those early sober years — and in Puerto Palma later — was a man named Lance Clearwater, who ran a fishing Instagram account called The Crappie Wrangler and traveled every year to a northwestern Mexican state well known for one particular industry that is not fishing, though Clearwater did fish there, with his father, and he brought friends from the recovery community along. This too is noted as geography. The state in question requires no elaboration.

The recovery world and the supplement industry overlapped in Sam's life the way two rivers overlap at a delta — separately sourced, converging without announcement. He worked in wholesale supplement distribution for approximately ten years, out of a small storefront in the northeastern United States. The dominant national distributor in that industry was a company called Continental Sports Wholesale. It no longer exists. His primary mentor was a man named Kenneth, who owned a health distribution company in the American Midwest and was, by Sam's account, a genuinely good person — the kind of man who, when a business associate named Adrian Holt attempted to file a fraudulent chargeback against a legitimate eighty-thousand-dollar transaction, simply wrote Holt a check out of his own pocket to make the problem go away.

Kenneth's business was not small. He managed distribution for a major national supplement chain, two major e-commerce platforms, and had acquired two legacy brands with decades of market history. The chain itself was subsequently purchased by a Chinese pharmaceutical conglomerate in 2020. In that same year, Kenneth did approximately one hundred million dollars in N95 masks and hand sanitizer on government contracts. He held active supply agreements with Nigeria, Bahrain, Jordan, and Saudi Arabia. He had, by his own account, broken bread with heads of state in the Gulf. Jordan's intelligence service is considered among the most capable in the world, with deep operational relationships across Western agencies. Kenneth's presence in this story is as a node, not a suspect. The document is explicit about that.

Adrian Holt, who tried the chargeback, lives in Puerto Palma.

The recovery industry had its own economy. Sam attended treatment twice at a facility in New England owned by a man named Edward Stafford — who arrived at his centers by helicopter, drove a sports car that cost more than most people's houses, and at his peak operated somewhere between twenty and thirty treatment centers before selling all of them. A friend of Sam's named J.J., who later died, spoke of Stafford with genuine admiration.

Craig Navarro's sponsor in recovery — not Sam's sponsor, Craig's — was a man named Mike Halley, who managed a sober living house in a small New England coastal town. The network Halley and others operated functioned as follows: families were persuaded to pay significant monthly fees to place a family member in the coastal house for thirty days. After thirty days, the residents were transferred to houses in either a Green Mountain city or the larger northeastern port city — houses operated by men like Jim Reilly and Tom Sheppard, who maintained eighteen beds in a three-unit residential building with no tenant rights framework, charging families roughly eighteen hundred dollars per resident per month. Reilly was separately charged with purchasing a urine analysis laboratory and submitting his own biological samples to it in order to fraudulently bill insurance companies. He was later indicted on narcotics trafficking charges.

A pattern runs through this network — not of crime, necessarily, though crime is documented — but of people who knew each other, who moved money and people across long distances through informal channels, who ended up in particular places, and some of whom ended up dead. Quietly. In their sleep. The document records this as a pattern of observation, not an allegation.

Now: Puerto Palma.

The property landscape of Puerto Palma is dominated by a single developer — call him Maxwell Greenfield. Greenfield operates a vacation rental company with a portfolio exceeding three hundred properties across the region. Sam lived in Greenfield properties on multiple occasions across multiple years: a house on the main road, a house on a sunset boulevard, a property in the hills, one in a suburb of the capital, and a riverfront complex called Riverwalk. This is market concentration. When one operator controls the majority of available rental housing in a geographic area, they control where people sleep.

The reason Sam is in Costa Rica — the Republic, as he thinks of it — at all connects to a specific individual: a former law enforcement officer from a midwestern American state who became a successful e-commerce seller, sold his business, and relocated to Playa Palmas, a residential development owned by Greenfield. Sam has known this man since 2012. They vacationed together. They did business together. They had a falling out — a deal went wrong, and Sam had a confrontation with a woman who was this man's wife's close friend — and the friendship fractured.

This man is an investor in a hotel in Puerto Palma called Villa Amara. He lives inside Greenfield's development. He had direct, sustained personal access to Greenfield. At some point during their friendship — specifically while intoxicated — he told Sam that Maxwell Greenfield had met with senior officials of the current presidential administration and the country's primary foreign intelligence agency over a period of approximately four years.

This is hearsay. The source was drunk. It is unverified. It is in the document because the source had the proximity to make the claim plausible, and because Greenfield's position — controlling the dominant share of housing in a town that functions as a waypoint for international travelers, American retirees, and the various social networks that converge in this story — is exactly the kind of position that attracts institutional interest. It is documented as a disclosure, not a fact.

The person who introduced Sam to the Republic — who is, in the most direct sense, why Sam is here — lives in Greenfield's compound and invested in Greenfield's neighbor's hotel.

Before the Republic, there was a sober living house called Hill View, run by a man named Bill Kenmore. Among Kenmore's business associates was a partner in a mortgage company that operated at the edge of legal practice. That partner died. In the months before his death, while his organs were failing, he told Sam directly: stay away from a man named Jake Talley. He is dangerous.

Sam had known Talley from the gym, from the recovery community, from the supplement store where Sam worked. Talley was a customer. He helped Sam move furniture. He was present in the way that certain people are present — useful, adjacent, embedded.

Years later, Sam moved into an apartment in a beachfront development in Puerto Palma called Breakwater, owned by a man named Marcus Lippincott. Lippincott subsequently relocated into Greenfield's personal residence at Playa Palmas. Greenfield moved out to make room for him. This is not a rental relationship. Lippincott's daughter-in-law is engaged to Jake Talley — the man a dying person specifically warned Sam to avoid, years earlier, in a different country.

The woman who introduced Sam to Lippincott's apartment had been his partner — call her Clara, a Venezuelan woman with a background in film production. Sam was forty-five days sober when they met. Clara arrived at his house with a cat. The cat disappeared when they moved into their first shared apartment — a Greenfield property on the main road. Within days, a kitten appeared behind the trash barrel. At the same time, a man named Jairo — Clara's friend — also appeared. Sam adopted the kitten. He cared for it for months. It was injured on barbed wire. He stressed over it. Clara eventually gave it away. The recipient was the wife of a man who had been sober for ten years. That wife subsequently left her husband for someone connected to Clara's social circle. She now has the cat and posts photographs of it on Instagram.

The cat is documentation of a chain of custody. The chain is verifiable.

Sam was in recovery. The Alcoholics Anonymous community in Puerto Palma is small and tight, as these communities are everywhere: shared vulnerability, regular meetings, known faces. It is also a perfect social penetration surface — membership is public by presence, attendance is predictable, and the emotional bonds formed there are genuine and load-bearing. The Hermosa Bungalows, where Sam and Clara first lived together, is owned by a long-standing member of that community. This is noted as social network geography, not accusation.

Meanwhile, almost everyone Sam knows in Puerto Palma has a connection to Mexico City. The capital of Mexico is also the Latin American operational headquarters of a major international religious organization — one whose door-to-door territory mapping, shift-based volunteer rotations, and absolute hierarchical discipline have been observed, in this case, operating from a camouflaged property approximately fifty meters from Sam's residence in Puerto Palma. The property features a raised terrace, artificial plastic foliage zip-tied to a chainlink fence — visually opaque, radio-frequency transparent — and a permanently parked silver sedan of a model common enough to attract no attention.

That religious organization's global Memorial attendance statistics, published annually, show a striking and geographically specific increase: millions of new members across sub-Saharan Africa — Congo, Ghana, Kenya, and others — in the most recent reporting year. The Canary Islands, specifically the island of Gran Canaria, have been documented as a primary transit corridor for sub-Saharan African and Somali migrants en route to mainland Europe. The organizational infrastructure required to move people at that scale through that corridor does not emerge spontaneously. It is placed here as context, not as accusation against any individual.

Now the technical layer.

The telecommunications distribution box at the edge of Sam's Puerto Palma property contains, on its interior surface, a handwritten annotation in field technician shorthand: a network access point designation, a fiber pigtail notation, and a date — June 21st, 2023. A physical modification to the fiber infrastructure serving that property, documented in the technician's own handwriting, predating every documented technical event in this case by months.

The company that maintains telecommunications infrastructure contracts across the Republic — specifically for generator equipment co-located with the national power and communications grid — is a small firm that also holds the exclusive domestic distribution rights for a British industrial control manufacturer whose flagship remote monitoring gateway maintains a permanent cellular uplink to servers in England, polling at four-second intervals regardless of whether the local network is active. The gateway's default administrative credentials across the national fleet: Admin, Password1234. Its industrial control protocol carries no authentication and no encryption, because the protocol was designed in 1979 for closed industrial networks, and nobody changed the defaults.

The residential router at Sam's Guácima property — a suburb of the capital where he lived for several months in late 2025 and early 2026 — was running a remote management protocol on a non-standard port documented in a configuration backup file that Sam found on his own device. The router's administrative password was remotely reset on January 30th, 2026. This protocol is a documented attack surface with multiple published vulnerability disclosures. Its exploitation by an ISP-level actor requires no sophisticated capability — only access to the provider's management server, which any contractor with a legitimate service badge could theoretically reach.

On January 27th, 2026, at 2:12 in the morning, a browser extension appeared in Sam's Chrome cache. Size: 8.3 megabytes. The extension was created while Sam was asleep, with no active browser sessions. A standard script of this type measures in kilobytes. 8.3 megabytes is an order of magnitude beyond normal. The corresponding browser history: none. A second script, registered in the browser under the name touch-communication, imported a base-64 encoding library. Base-64 has one primary use: encoding binary data — audio, images, keystrokes — as text for transmission. Running as a hidden background process, it is a remote access bridge.

Two injected scripts appeared in Sam's browsers at the Guácima property. One originated from a Colombian domain variant of a major short-term rental platform — a domain Sam had never visited. The other was traced to the website of the telecommunications infrastructure contractor. Two companies. One managing the national communications backbone. One operating the property where Sam slept. Both appearing as script sources in a browser that had never contacted either domain.

The property had six power outlets installed behind the kitchen sink, all glowing red or green from inside. Standard residential outlets contain no internal light source. This was documented on video. Workers arrived during Sam's residency and installed components while he was present. What they installed: diodes, and light-based data transmission injection points — devices that encode information in imperceptible modulations of visible or infrared light, producing no radio frequency signature detectable by standard network scanning tools.

The ceiling of one particular property — the one he returned to after a long absence, owned by people connected to the network — was, on his return, approximately three feet lower than it had been before. The renovation had occurred in the interval. Three feet of concealed overhead space is sufficient for antenna arrays, directional acoustic hardware, power distribution, and sensor clusters. It cannot be audited without demolition.

On the telephone pole across the street from that same property: twenty-eight cameras.

---

At 2:13 in the morning on April 29th, 2026, Sam recorded an aerial platform over the northern end of Puerto Palma. Two clips, sixty-two seconds apart. The platform maintained position, then repositioned — controlled station-keeping, not drift. Acoustic analysis resolved the propulsion signature: three-blade configuration, approximately seven thousand eighty revolutions per minute. This band is consistent with a professional-grade aerial surveillance platform or a consumer drone modified with aftermarket stealth propellers designed to reduce acoustic output. Single white-blue point of light. No navigation lights. No registered flight plan.

The same light type had been photographed on two previous dates months earlier, across two different locations.

Three months earlier, on a recording made at his current residence, a Fourier transform analysis of the audio revealed the following: a frequency of 46.875 hertz — the sampling clock rate of a digital signal processing system running at 48 kilohertz divided by 1024 — and its harmonics through the fifth: 93.75, 140.625, 187.5, and 234.375 hertz. The second harmonic was the strongest signal in the recording. This is the acoustic fingerprint of a non-sinusoidal periodic waveform at 46.875 hertz — the same frequency documented at the first property in Puerto Palma, at the Riverwalk property, and at the Guácima property. Three provinces. Three separate electrical grids. One frequency.

A digital system that processes the world at 46.875 hertz is a digital system that is processing the world.

Sam filed a complaint with the national investigative police in January 2026. It was received, routed through official channels, and forwarded to the local substation covering the jurisdiction where the properties were located — the same jurisdiction where the alleged operators lived, worked, and held national government infrastructure contracts.

He sent a fourteen-page technical briefing to the national power authority regarding a confirmed cyberattack against their infrastructure — a backdoor written in C, command-and-control routed through a major productivity platform's API, attributed to a Chinese state-aligned threat actor with confirmed presence across fifty-three organizations in forty-two countries. The power authority replied with instructions for reporting a power outage.

The record exists. It has been transmitted to over a hundred and fifty recipients across nine institutional categories. It is dated. It is timestamped. It has been routed through official channels in at least two countries.

The frequency is still there.

46.875 hertz. The rate at which a digital system samples the world it is monitoring.

The system is still running.

So is Sam.

So is this document.
